🔬 Dataset & Methodology

Dataset

The analysis uses 677 unique Kalshi ticker markets for Austin daily high-temperature contracts (KXHIGHAUS / HIGHAUS), all of which have fully settled (finalized with a YES or NO outcome). Of these, 210 won (settled YES) and 467 lost (settled NO).

The source files are intraday_analysis.json and unbiased_analysis.json in the Kalshi history directory. These files were generated from the Kalshi API and contain, for each ticker, a field called bins_hit — a list of every $0.05 price level that the ticker traded at during its entire lifetime.

Why "bins_hit" instead of "last_price"?

The previous analysis used last_price_dollars — the final traded price before settlement. This creates a severe sampling bias: by the time a market settles, prices cluster at extremes ($0.01 or $0.99) because the outcome is nearly known. The mid-range ($0.50–$0.80) had only 10–50 observations each, far too few for significance.

bins_hit solves this by asking: "At any point during trading, when this ticker's price was at $X, did it ultimately win?" This gives us 100–300 observations per price level — enough for statistical testing.

What this measures: If you see a bucket currently trading at $0.80, historical data says that 85.2% of all buckets that ever traded at $0.80 ultimately settled YES. You're buying at $0.80 something that's actually worth $0.852 — a $0.052 edge per contract.

Methodology

For each price level ($0.05, $0.10, ..., $0.95):

  • Touched: Count how many unique tickers ever traded at this price level
  • Won: Of those, how many ultimately settled YES
  • Win Rate: Won ÷ Touched
  • Implied Probability: The buy price itself (e.g., $0.80 = 80% implied)
  • Edge: Win Rate − Implied Probability
  • EV per contract: Win Rate × (1 − Price) − (1 − Win Rate) × Price = Win Rate − Price
  • Significance: One-sided z-test against H₀: true win rate = implied probability, with Wilson score 95% confidence intervals

Why This Reasoning Is Sound

  • Large sample sizes: Each price level has 41–300 independent observations, well above the n=30 threshold for the central limit theorem
  • Real outcomes: Every observation is a fully settled market with a definitive YES/NO result — no simulation, no modeling, no assumptions about weather
  • No lookahead bias: The bins_hit field records prices that occurred during trading, before settlement. The question "did this market ultimately win?" uses only the final settlement
  • Conservative testing: Using one-sided z-tests with p < 0.05 significance threshold, plus Wilson confidence intervals which are more accurate than Wald intervals for proportions
  • Deduplicated: Each ticker is counted once per price level, even if it traded at that level multiple times
⚠️ Caveats:
  • Survivorship in bins_hit: A ticker that quickly passes through $0.50 on its way to $0.95 still counts as "touched $0.50." This may overcount transient prices.
  • Execution gap: Seeing a price of $0.80 doesn't mean you can buy at $0.80 — the bid-ask spread and available liquidity matter. Your actual fill price may be higher.
  • n drops above $0.65: At $0.80 there are 54 observations, at $0.85 only 41. These are statistically meaningful but would benefit from more data.
  • This dataset covers ~1,042 event dates. Seasonal or market-structure changes over time could affect future results.
